EER extends ER by adding concepts like subclasses, superclasses, specialization, generalization, and categories to better model real-world complexity.

🔷 Subclasses and Superclasses

An entity type may have additional meaningful subgroupings of its entities. EER diagrams extend ER diagrams to represent these subgroupings, called subclasses or subtypes.

Example – EMPLOYEE subgroupings:
  • By Job type: SECRETARY, ENGINEER, TECHNICIAN
  • By role: MANAGER (employees who manage)
  • By pay method: SALARIED_EMPLOYEE, HOURLY_EMPLOYEE

Each subgrouping is a subset of EMPLOYEE entities → each is a subclass. EMPLOYEE is the superclass for all of them.

Superclass/Subclass Relationships
  • EMPLOYEE / SECRETARY
  • EMPLOYEE / TECHNICIAN
  • EMPLOYEE / MANAGER
IS-A Relationships
  • SECRETARY IS-A EMPLOYEE
  • TECHNICIAN IS-A EMPLOYEE
  • MANAGER IS-A EMPLOYEE
Key Rules about Subclass Membership:
  • A subclass member is the same entity in a distinct specific role.
  • An entity cannot exist in the database merely by being a member of a subclass — it must also be a member of the superclass.
  • A member of the superclass can be optionally included as a member of any number of its subclasses.
  • It is not necessary that every entity in a superclass be a member of some subclass.
Multi-membership Example:
  • A salaried engineer → belongs to ENGINEER and SALARIED_EMPLOYEE (2 subclasses).
  • A salaried engineering manager → belongs to MANAGER, ENGINEER, and SALARIED_EMPLOYEE (3 subclasses).

🧬 Attribute Inheritance

An entity that is a member of a subclass inherits:

All Attributes of the entity as a member of the superclass.
All Relationships of the entity as a member of the superclass.
Example: SECRETARY (as well as TECHNICIAN and ENGINEER) inherits the attributes Name, SSN, Birth_date, Address, etc. from EMPLOYEE. Every SECRETARY entity will have values for these inherited attributes.

🔽 Specialization

Definition: Specialization is the process of defining a set of subclasses of a superclass. The set of subclasses is based upon some distinguishing characteristics of the entities in the superclass.

Example: {SECRETARY, ENGINEER, TECHNICIAN} is a specialization of EMPLOYEE based upon job type.

A superclass may have several specializations.

Three specializations of EMPLOYEE:
  • {SECRETARY, TECHNICIAN, ENGINEER} — based on job type
  • {MANAGER} — based on management role
  • {HOURLY_EMPLOYEE, SALARIED_EMPLOYEE} — based on pay method

Two Main Reasons for Specialization

Reason 1: Specific Attributes Certain attributes may apply to some but not all entities of the superclass.
Reason 2: Specific Relationships Some relationship types may be participated in only by members of the subclass.

Example: Only HOURLY_EMPLOYEEs can belong to a TRADE_UNION via the BELONGS_TO relationship.

Specialization Process

  • Define a set of subclasses of an entity type.
  • Establish additional specific attributes with each subclass.
  • Establish additional specific relationship types between each subclass and other entity types or other subclasses.

🔼 Generalization

Definition: Generalization is the reverse of specialization. Several classes with common features are generalized into a superclass; the original classes become its subclasses.
Example: CAR and TRUCK are generalized into VEHICLE.
  • Both CAR and TRUCK become subclasses of VEHICLE.
  • Common attributes (Vehicle_id, Price, License_plate_no) move to VEHICLE.
  • Specific attributes remain: Max_speed, No_of_passengers → CAR; No_of_axles, Tonnage → TRUCK.
We can view CAR and TRUCK as a specialization of VEHICLE, or alternatively, VEHICLE as a generalization of CAR and TRUCK. Both perspectives describe the same end-result structure.
Diagrammatic Notation Note Arrow pointing to the generalized superclass = generalization. Arrows pointing to specialized subclasses = specialization. In practice, this distinction is often subjective — advice is not to draw any arrows.
A superclass or subclass represents a collection (set/grouping) of entities and is shown as a rectangle in EER diagrams, just like entity types. All entity types, superclasses, and subclasses can collectively be called classes.

⚙️ Constraints on Specialization & Generalization

1. Membership Condition Constraints

Predicate-Defined (Condition-Defined) Membership in the subclass is determined by a condition on an attribute of the superclass. The condition is written next to the line attaching the subclass to the superclass in an EER diagram.
Attribute-Defined Specialization If all subclasses in a specialization have their membership condition on the same attribute of the superclass, the specialization is called attribute-defined. That attribute is the defining attribute.

Example: job_type is the defining attribute for SECRETARY, TECHNICIAN, ENGINEER of EMPLOYEE.
User-Defined: If no condition determines membership, the subclass is user-defined. Membership is specified individually for each entity in the superclass by the user.

2. Disjointness Constraint

Disjoint "d" An entity can be a member of at most one subclass of the specialization. Shown by letter "d" in EER diagram.
Overlapping "o" The same entity may be a member of more than one subclass of the specialization. Shown by letter "o" in EER diagram.

3. Completeness Constraint

Total (Double Line) Every entity in the superclass must be a member of at least one subclass. Shown by a double line in EER diagrams.
Partial (Single Line) An entity is allowed not to belong to any of the subclasses. Shown by a single line in EER diagrams.

4 Possible Constraint Combinations

Disjoint, Total Each entity belongs to exactly one subclass.
Disjoint, Partial Each entity belongs to at most one subclass (or none).
Overlapping, Total Each entity belongs to at least one subclass (possibly many).
Overlapping, Partial Each entity may belong to multiple or no subclasses.

📝 Insertion and Deletion Rules

  • Deleting an entity from a superclass implies it is automatically deleted from all the subclasses to which it belongs.
  • Inserting an entity in a superclass implies that the entity is mandatorily inserted in all predicate-defined (or attribute-defined) subclasses for which the entity satisfies the defining predicate.
  • Inserting an entity in a superclass of a total specialization implies that the entity is mandatorily inserted in at least one of the subclasses of the specialization.

🌐 Hierarchies, Lattices & Shared Subclasses

A subclass may itself have further subclasses specified on it — forming a hierarchy or a lattice.

Specialization Hierarchy Every subclass has only one superclass → called single inheritance. This forms a tree structure.
Specialization Lattice A subclass can be a subclass of more than one superclass → called multiple inheritance. A subclass with more than one superclass is called a shared subclass.
In a lattice or hierarchy, a subclass inherits attributes not only of its direct superclass, but also of all its predecessor superclasses.
Example — ENGINEERING_MANAGER shared subclass: It is a subclass of both ENGINEER and MANAGER (and SALARIED_EMPLOYEE), inheriting attributes from all three. This forms a specialization lattice.
UNIVERSITY lattice example: PERSON → EMPLOYEE, ALUMNUS, STUDENT. EMPLOYEE → STAFF, FACULTY, STUDENT_ASSISTANT. STUDENT → GRADUATE_STUDENT, UNDERGRADUATE_STUDENT. STUDENT_ASSISTANT → RESEARCH_ASSISTANT, TEACHING_ASSISTANT. This demonstrates multiple levels with multiple inheritance.

Design Approaches

Top-Down (Specialization) Start with an entity type, then define subclasses by successive specialization. Called a top-down conceptual refinement process.
Bottom-Up (Generalization) Start with many entity types and generalize those that have common properties. Called a bottom-up conceptual synthesis process.

In practice, a combination of both processes is usually employed.

🔗 Categories (UNION Types)

Definition: A category (or UNION type) is a subclass that represents a subset of the union of multiple superclasses from different entity types.
Example — Vehicle Registration Database:
A vehicle owner can be a PERSON, a BANK (holding a lease), or a COMPANY. A category OWNER is created to represent a subset of the union of COMPANY ∪ BANK ∪ PERSON. A category member must exist in at least one of its superclasses.

Category vs. Shared Subclass

Category (UNION type) Subset of the union of its superclasses. Member must exist in at least one superclass. Superclasses can be different entity types. Shown with "U" in EER diagram.
Shared Subclass Subset of the intersection of its superclasses. Member must exist in all of its superclasses. Leads to multiple inheritance.

📐 Formal Definitions of the EER Model

Class C A set or collection of entities. Includes any EER schema construct that groups entities: entity types, subclasses, superclasses, and categories.
Subclass S of C Inherits all attributes and relationships of class C. Set of entities always a subset: S ⊆ C. C is called the superclass of S. Relationship denoted C/S (IS-A).
Specialization Z Z = {S1, S2, …, Sn} is a set of subclasses with the same superclass G. G is called a generalization of the subclasses.
Total Specialization Z is total if: S1 ∪ S2 ∪ … ∪ Sn = G. (Every entity in G belongs to at least one subclass.) Otherwise, Z is partial.
Disjoint Specialization Z is disjoint if: Si ∩ Sj = ∅ for i ≠ j. (No entity belongs to more than one subclass.) Otherwise, Z is overlapping.
Predicate-Defined Subclass Subclass S of C is predicate-defined if a predicate (condition) p on attributes of C determines membership: S = C[p], where C[p] is the set of entities in C that satisfy condition p.
User-Defined Subclass A subclass not defined by a predicate. Membership is specified individually for each entity in the superclass by the user.
Category / UNION Type T — Formal Definition:
T is a class that is a subset of the union of n defining superclasses D1, D2, …, Dn (n > 1):
T ⊆ (D1 ∪ D2 ∪ … ∪ Dn)

A predicate pi can be specified on each Di. If a predicate is specified on every Di:
T = (D1[p1] ∪ D2[p2] ∪ … ∪ Dn[pn])

📊 Alternative Diagrammatic Notations & UML

ER/EER diagrams are one specific notation for displaying conceptual data model constructs. DB design tools use many alternative notations for the same or similar concepts.

One popular alternative: UML Class Diagrams. In UML, classes correspond to entity types, with attributes and operations shown inside the class box. Inheritance (specialization/generalization) is shown with a hollow triangle arrow pointing to the superclass.

Alternative Symbol Comparisons

Entity type/class EER: rectangle. UML: class box with sections.
Attribute EER: oval. UML: listed inside class box. Also shown as line with circle.
Relationship EER: diamond. UML: line between classes. Also shown as rectangle or plain line.
Cardinality Notation Variants:
  • 1 and N (standard EER)
  • (O,n) and (1,1) — min/max notation
  • 1..1 and 0..n — UML style
  • Crow's foot notation (various tools)

Specialization/Generalization Notation Variants

EER Notation Circle with "d" (disjoint) or "o" (overlapping). Single line = partial, double line = total. Subclasses in rectangles connected by lines through the circle.
UML Notation Hollow triangle pointing up to superclass (generalization arrow). Subclasses shown beneath. Constraints labeled {disjoint}, {overlapping}, {complete}, {incomplete}.

📌 Quick Reference — Key Terms

Superclass The higher-level entity type from which subclasses inherit.
Subclass / Subtype A meaningful subgrouping of a superclass (S ⊆ C).
IS-A Relationship Denotes superclass/subclass: e.g., SECRETARY IS-A EMPLOYEE.
Specialization Top-down process of defining subclasses from a superclass.
Generalization Bottom-up process of creating a superclass from similar entity types.
Attribute Inheritance A subclass member inherits all attributes and relationships of the superclass.
Predicate-Defined Subclass membership determined by a condition on a superclass attribute.
Attribute-Defined All subclasses in a specialization use the same defining attribute.
Disjoint ("d") Entity can belong to at most one subclass.
Overlapping ("o") Entity can belong to more than one subclass.
Total (double line) Every superclass entity must be in at least one subclass.
Partial (single line) Superclass entity may not belong to any subclass.
Hierarchy Single inheritance — each subclass has exactly one superclass (tree).
Lattice Multiple inheritance — subclass can have more than one superclass.
Shared Subclass A subclass with more than one superclass (multiple inheritance).
Category / UNION Type Subclass as a subset of the union of multiple different-type superclasses.
