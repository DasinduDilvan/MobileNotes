1. Translating English Sentences

System and software engineers routinely convert natural language requirements into precise logical statements. The process follows two main steps:

  • Step 1: Identify atomic propositions and assign propositional variables to each.
  • Step 2: Determine the appropriate logical connectives (¬, ∧, ∨, →, ↔) that connect them.
Example 1 — Simple Translation
"If I go to the saloon or to the gym, I will not go shopping."

Variable assignments: p = I go to the saloon  |  q = I go to the gym  |  r = I will go shopping

If p or q, then not r
(p ∨ q) → ¬r
Example 2 — "Only if" Translation
"You can access the Internet from campus only if you are a computer science major or you are not a freshman."

Variable assignments: a = You can access the internet from campus  |  c = You are a computer science major  |  f = You are a freshman

a → (c ∨ ¬f)
Example 3 — "Unless" Translation (In-class)
"You cannot ride the roller coaster if you are under 4 feet tall unless you are older than 16 years old."

Variable assignments: q = You can ride the roller coaster  |  r = You are under 4 feet tall  |  s = You are older than 16 years old

(r ∧ ¬s) → ¬q

2. System Specifications

System and software engineers take requirements stated in English and express them in a precise specification language based on logic. This ensures requirements are unambiguous and can be checked for consistency.

Example — Automated Reply System
"The automated reply cannot be sent when the file system is full."

Variable assignments: p = The automated reply can be sent  |  q = The file system is full

q → ¬p
Consistent System Specifications
Definition: A list of propositions is consistent if it is possible to assign truth values to the propositional variables so that each proposition is simultaneously true.

Exercise: Are the following specifications consistent?

  • "The diagnostic message is stored in the buffer, or it is retransmitted."
  • "The diagnostic message is not stored in the buffer."
  • "If the diagnostic message is stored in the buffer, then it is retransmitted."
Solution

Let p = "The diagnostic message is stored in the buffer" and q = "The diagnostic message is retransmitted."

The three specifications translate to: p ∨ q, ¬p, p → q.

When p = false and q = true, all three statements are true. Therefore, the specification is consistent.
What if a fourth statement is added: "The diagnostic message is not retransmitted." (i.e., ¬q)?

Now there is no satisfying truth assignment. The specification becomes inconsistent.

3. Boolean Searching

Logical connectives are used extensively in searches of large collections of information, such as indexes of web pages. Because these searches employ techniques from propositional logic, they are called Boolean searches.

AND Matches records that contain both of two search terms. AND takes precedence over OR.
OR Matches records that contain one or both of two search terms.
NOT (AND NOT) Excludes a particular search term from results. In Google, written as -.
Boolean Search Examples
GoalSearch QueryWhat it Returns
Universities in New Mexico NEW AND MEXICO AND UNIVERSITIES Pages containing all three words (may include "new universities in Mexico")
Universities in New Mexico OR Arizona (NEW AND MEXICO OR ARIZONA) AND UNIVERSITIES Pages with UNIVERSITIES and either (NEW + MEXICO) or ARIZONA
Universities in Mexico, not New Mexico (MEXICO AND UNIVERSITIES) NOT NEW Pages with MEXICO and UNIVERSITIES but without the word NEW
Exact phrase search "New Mexico" AND UNIVERSITIES More precise — treats "New Mexico" as a single phrase
Note on Google: In Google, the word "AND" is not needed (all search terms are included by default). The word "NOT" is replaced by the symbol -. Example: MEXICO UNIVERSITIES -NEW.

4. Logic Puzzles

Puzzles that can be solved using logical reasoning are known as logic puzzles. Solving logic puzzles is an excellent way to practice working with the rules of logic. Computer programs designed to carry out logical reasoning often use well-known logic puzzles to illustrate their capabilities.

These puzzles were popularized by logician and philosopher Raymond Smullyan (Born 1919).

Knights and Knaves Puzzle
Setup: An island has two kinds of inhabitants — knights, who always tell the truth, and knaves, who always lie.

You go to the island and meet A and B.
  • A says: "B is a knight."
  • B says: "The two of us are of opposite types."

Question: What are the types of A and B?
Solution

Let p = "A is a knight" and q = "B is a knight." Then ¬p = A is a knave and ¬q = B is a knave.

  • Case 1 — Assume A is a knight (p = true): Then A tells the truth, so q must be true (B is a knight). If both are knights, B's statement "(p ∧ ¬q) ∨ (¬p ∧ q)" would have to be true, but it is not (both are the same type). Contradiction — so A is not a knight.
  • Case 2 — Assume A is a knave (¬p = true): Since A lies, B must not be a knight. So ¬q is also true. Both A and B are knaves. B's statement ("we are opposite types") is false, which is consistent with B being a knave.
Conclusion: Both A and B are knaves.
Muddy Children Puzzle (Example 02)
A father tells his two children (a boy and a girl) to play without getting dirty. Both get mud on their foreheads. The father says "At least one of you has a muddy forehead," then asks twice: "Do you know whether you have a muddy forehead?" (Children answer simultaneously; each can see the other's forehead but not their own.)

Solution: Let s = the son has a muddy forehead and d = the daughter has a muddy forehead. The father states s ∨ d is true.

  • First time asked: Both children answer "No" — the son sees mud on the daughter's forehead but cannot determine his own; same for the daughter.
  • After the first "No": The daughter reasons: "If d were false, the son (knowing s ∨ d) would have concluded s = true and answered Yes. Since he said No, d must be true." The son reasons symmetrically.
  • Second time asked: Both children answer "Yes".

5. Logic Circuits (Studied in depth in Later Chapter)

Electronic circuits process information as binary signals. Each input/output signal is viewed as a 0 (False) or 1 (True). Complex circuits are constructed from three fundamental building blocks called gates.

Inverter (NOT Gate) Takes a single input bit and produces its negation.
Input: p → Output: ¬p
OR Gate Takes two input bits and produces their disjunction.
Inputs: p, q → Output: p ∨ q
AND Gate Takes two input bits and produces their conjunction.
Inputs: p, q → Output: p ∧ q
Constructing Complex Circuits

More complicated digital circuits are built by combining these basic gates. To construct a circuit for an output expression, build a sub-circuit for each piece and then combine them using further gates.

Principle: Any Boolean expression can be implemented as a combination of NOT, OR, and AND gates by following the structure of the expression tree.
Worked Example 1

The AND gate takes inputs p and ¬q (¬q from an inverter on q) and produces p ∧ ¬q. The OR gate then takes p ∧ ¬q and ¬r (¬r from an inverter on r) to produce the final output:

(p ∧ ¬q) ∨ ¬r
Worked Example 2

Build a circuit that produces the following output from input bits p, q, and r:

(p ∨ ¬r) ∧ (¬p ∨ (q ∨ ¬r))

This requires two main sub-circuits feeding into a final AND gate. The upper path computes p ∨ ¬r; the lower path computes ¬p ∨ (q ∨ ¬r). The final AND gate combines both.

Practice Problems — Find the Output
Circuit (a) — Inputs: p, q, r

The q and r inputs pass through inverters (¬q, ¬r). An AND gate combines p and (q ∨ ¬r), followed by a NOT gate on the output.

¬(p ∧ (q ∨ ¬r))
Circuit (b) — Inputs: p, q, r

p and q each pass through inverters. (¬p ∧ ¬q) is combined with (p ∧ r) via an OR gate.

((¬p) ∧ (¬q)) ∨ (p ∧ r)
Construction Problem

Construct a combinatorial circuit using inverters, OR gates, and AND gates that produces the output from input bits p, q, and r:

((¬p ∨ ¬r) ∧ ¬q) ∨ (¬p ∧ (q ∨ r))

The circuit requires inverters on p, q, and r. Two AND-gate sub-circuits feed into a final OR gate.

6. AI Diagnosis Method (Optional)

This approach is from Artificial Intelligence: Foundations of Computational Agents by David Poole and Alan Mackworth (2010). It demonstrates how propositional logic can be applied to diagnose faults in physical systems.

Core Concepts
Knowledge Base (KB) A representation in propositional logic of the features of a piece of machinery or circuitry required for normal operation to produce observable outputs.
Observations Propositions representing the features that the system is currently exhibiting (what we can actually measure/see).
Assumables Atoms assumed to be true unless forced otherwise. Represent the assumption that components (switches, lights, circuit breakers) are functioning correctly.
Electrical System Description

The example system consists of: lights l1, l2 — wires w0, w1, w2, w3, w4 — switches s1, s2, s3 — circuit breaker cb1.

Knowledge Base Rules
English DescriptionLogical Formula
We have outside powerlive_outside
l1 and l2 are lightslight_l1, light_l2
If w0 is live, then l1 is livelive_w0 → live_l1
If w1 live, s2 up, s2 ok → w0 livelive_w1 ∧ up_s2 ∧ ok_s2 → live_w0
If w2 live, s2 down, s2 ok → w0 livelive_w2 ∧ down_s2 ∧ ok_s2 → live_w0
If w3 live, s1 up, s1 ok → w1 livelive_w3 ∧ up_s1 ∧ ok_s1 → live_w1
If w3 live, s1 down, s1 ok → w2 livelive_w3 ∧ down_s1 ∧ ok_s1 → live_w2
If w4 is live, then l2 is livelive_w4 → live_l2
If w3 live, s3 up, s3 ok → w4 livelive_w3 ∧ up_s3 ∧ ok_s3 → live_w4
If outside power and cb1 ok → w3 livelive_outside ∧ ok_cb1 → live_w3
l1 light, live, ok → l1 litlight_l1 ∧ live_l1 ∧ ok_l1 → lit_l1
l2 light, live, ok → l2 litlight_l2 ∧ live_l2 ∧ ok_l2 → lit_l2
Observations Added to the KB
Both switches up up_s1   up_s2
Both lights are dark ¬ lit_l1   ¬ lit_l2
Diagnosis Method
  • We assume all components are working correctly (assumables = true) unless forced to conclude otherwise.
  • Assumables: ok_cb1, ok_s1, ok_s2, ok_s3, ok_l1, ok_l2
  • If the system is working correctly (all assumables true), the KB and observations should be consistent.
  • The augmented KB is not consistent when all assumables are true — switches are both up, but lights are dark. Therefore, some assumables must be false.
Definition — Diagnosis: A diagnosis is a minimal set of assumables that must be false in order to explain the current observations of the system.
Diagnostic Results

The consistency-based diagnosis method yields 7 possible faults in the system. At least one of the following must hold:

  • Circuit Breaker 1 is not ok.
  • Both Switch 1 and Switch 2 are not ok.
  • Both Switch 1 and Light 2 are not ok.
  • Both Switch 2 and Switch 3 are not ok.
  • Both Switch 2 and Light 2 are not ok.
  • Both Light 1 and Switch 3 are not ok.
  • Both Light 1 and Light 2 are not ok.
For full details on this method, see: Artificial Intelligence: Foundations of Computational Agents — David Poole & Alan Mackworth, 2010.
