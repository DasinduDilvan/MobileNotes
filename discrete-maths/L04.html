1. Introduction & Core Definitions

An important type of step used in a mathematical argument is the replacement of a statement with another statement with the same truth value. Methods that produce propositions with the same truth value as a given compound proposition are used extensively in the construction of mathematical arguments.

Three Types of Propositions
Tautology A proposition that is always true, regardless of the truth values of its variables.
Example: p ∨ ¬p
Contradiction A proposition that is always false, regardless of the truth values of its variables.
Example: p ∧ ¬p
Contingency A proposition that is neither a tautology nor a contradiction — its truth value depends on the variables.

2. Logical Equivalence

Definition: Two compound propositions p and q are logically equivalent if p ↔ q is a tautology. We write this as p ⇔ q or p ≡ q.

Two compound propositions are equivalent if and only if the columns in a truth table giving their truth values agree for all combinations of input variables.

3. De Morgan's Laws  — Augustus De Morgan (1806–1871)

De Morgan's Laws describe how negation distributes over conjunction and disjunction.

First Law — Negate an AND
¬(p ∧ q) ≡ ¬p ∨ ¬q

The negation of an "and" statement is the "or" of the negations.

Second Law — Negate an OR
¬(p ∨ q) ≡ ¬p ∧ ¬q

The negation of an "or" statement is the "and" of the negations.

Verification — De Morgan's Second Law
pq¬p¬qp ∨ q¬(p ∨ q)¬p ∧ ¬q

Applying De Morgan's Laws — English Example
Original: "Miguel has a cellphone and he has a laptop computer." → p ∧ q

Negation: By De Morgan's First Law, ¬(p ∧ q) ≡ ¬p ∨ ¬q

Result: "Miguel does not have a cellphone or he does not have a laptop computer."

5. Equivalence Proofs

We can show two expressions are logically equivalent by developing a series of logically equivalent statements, beginning with A and ending with B:

A ≡ A₁ ≡ A₂ ≡ ... ≡ Aₙ ≡ B

Whenever a proposition (represented by a propositional variable) occurs in the equivalences listed, it may be replaced by an arbitrarily complex compound proposition.

6. Normal Forms (Optional)

Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)
Definition: A propositional formula is in Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF) if it consists of a disjunction of disjuncts, where each disjunct consists of a conjunction of atomic formulas or negations of atomic formulas.

Structure: (literal ∧ literal ∧ ...) ∨ (literal ∧ literal ∧ ...) ∨ ...
✅ In DNF

(p ∧ ¬q) ∨ (¬p ∨ q)

Disjunction of conjunctions of literals.

❌ Not in DNF

p ∧ (p ∨ q)

Contains a conjunction with a disjunction inside — not allowed in DNF.

How to construct DNF from a truth table: Build the disjunction with one disjunct per row where the formula evaluates to T. Each disjunct is a conjunction of all variables — using the positive form if the variable is T in that row, and the negated form if the variable is F. DNF is important for circuit design methods.
DNF Example — Find DNF of (p ∨ q) → ¬r

This proposition is true when r is false, or when both p and q are false.

(¬p ∧ ¬q) ∨ ¬r
Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)
Definition: A compound proposition is in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) if it is a conjunction of disjunctions (also called clauses).

Structure: (literal ∨ literal ∨ ...) ∧ (literal ∨ literal ∨ ...) ∧ ...
  • Every proposition can be put in an equivalent CNF.
  • CNF is obtained by: (1) eliminating implications, (2) moving negation inwards, (3) applying distributive and associative laws.
  • CNF is important in resolution theorem proving used in Artificial Intelligence (AI).
CNF Example — Put ¬(p → q) ∨ (r → p) into CNF
¬(p → q) ∨ (r → p)Original expression Step 1: Eliminate implication signs ¬(¬p ∨ q) ∨ (¬r ∨ p)using p → q ≡ ¬p ∨ q Step 2: Move negation inwards; eliminate double negation (p ∧ ¬q) ∨ (¬r ∨ p)De Morgan + double negation Step 3: Convert to CNF using associative/distributive laws (p ∨ ¬r ∨ p) ∧ (¬q ∨ ¬r ∨ p)Distribute ∨ over ∧
(p ∨ ¬r ∨ p) ∧ (¬q ∨ ¬r ∨ p)

7. Propositional Satisfiability

Satisfiable: A compound proposition is satisfiable if there is at least one assignment of truth values to its variables that makes it true.
Unsatisfiable: A compound proposition is unsatisfiable if no assignment of truth values makes it true. This is equivalent to saying its negation is a tautology.
Example — Determine Satisfiability of Three Propositions
Proposition 1: (p ∨ ¬q) ∧ (q ∨ ¬r) ∧ (r ∨ ¬p)
Proposition 2: (p ∨ q ∨ r) ∧ (¬p ∨ ¬q ∨ ¬r)
Proposition 3: (p ∨ ¬q) ∧ (q ∨ ¬r) ∧ (r ∨ ¬p) ∧ (p ∨ q ∨ r) ∧ (¬p ∨ ¬q ∨ ¬r)
Solution (Reasoning without truth tables)
Proposition 1 — Satisfiable ✓

True when p, q, and r all have the same truth value (all T or all F). So it is satisfiable.

Proposition 2 — Satisfiable ✓

True when at least one of p, q, r is true AND at least one is false. So it is satisfiable.

Proposition 3 — Unsatisfiable ✗

For Proposition 1 (part of 3) to be true: all three variables must have the same truth value. For Proposition 2 (part of 3) to be true: at least one must be true and at least one must be false. These two conditions are contradictory — they cannot both hold simultaneously. Therefore Proposition 3 is unsatisfiable.

Useful Notation
⋁ⁿⱼ₌₁ pⱼ   is used for   p₁ ∨ p₂ ∨ ... ∨ pₙ

⋀ⁿⱼ₌₁ pⱼ   is used for   p₁ ∧ p₂ ∧ ... ∧ pₙ

8. Sudoku as a Satisfiability Problem

Sudoku (Japanese: suuji wa dokushin ni kagiru) means "the digits must remain single."
Structure of a Sudoku Puzzle

A Sudoku puzzle is represented by a 9×9 grid made up of nine 3×3 subgrids (called blocks). Some of the 81 cells are pre-assigned one of the numbers 1–9. The puzzle is solved by assigning numbers to blank cells so that every row, every column, and every block contains each of the nine numbers exactly once.

Encoding as a Satisfiability Problem

Let p(i, j, n) denote the proposition that is true when the number n is in the cell in the ith row and jth column.

There are 9 × 9 × 9 = 729 such propositional variables.
For example, in a sample puzzle: p(5,1,6) is true, but p(5,j,6) is false for j = 2, 3, …, 9.
The Four Types of Assertions Required
AssertionLogical Formula
For each pre-filled cell with value n in row i, column j assert p(i, j, n)
Every row contains every number ⋀⁹ᵢ₌₁ ⋀⁹ₙ₌₁ ⋁⁹ⱼ₌₁ p(i, j, n)
Every column contains every number ⋀⁹ⱼ₌₁ ⋀⁹ₙ₌₁ ⋁⁹ᵢ₌₁ p(i, j, n)
Each 3×3 block contains every number ⋀²ᵣ₌₀ ⋀²ₛ₌₀ ⋀⁹ₙ₌₁ ⋀³ᵢ₌₁ ⋁³ⱼ₌₁ p(3r+i, 3s+j, n)
No cell contains more than one number For all i, j, n, n' where n ≠ n': p(i,j,n) → ¬p(i,j,n')
Solving the Problem

To solve a Sudoku puzzle, we need to find an assignment of truth values to the 729 variables p(i,j,n) that makes the conjunction of all the above assertions true. Those variables assigned T yield the solution to the puzzle.

Computational Challenge: A truth table can always be used to determine satisfiability, but this approach is computationally intractable for large problems, even for modern computers. There has been significant research into developing efficient SAT-solving algorithms, as many practical problems can be translated into satisfiability problems.
