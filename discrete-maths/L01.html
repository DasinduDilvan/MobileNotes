🔢 What is Discrete Mathematics?

Definition: Discrete Mathematics is a collection of mathematical topics that examine and use finite or countably infinite mathematical objects.

Key distinction: Discrete means consisting of distinct or unconnected elements — it is the opposite of continuous mathematics.


Problems solved using Discrete Mathematics include:

  • How many ways are there to choose a valid password on a computer system?
  • What is the probability of winning a lottery?
  • Is there a link between two computers in a network?
  • How can I identify spam email messages?
  • How can I encrypt a message so that no unintended recipient can read it?
  • What is the shortest path between two cities using a transportation system?
  • How can a list of integers be sorted in increasing order? How many steps are required?
  • How can a circuit that adds two integers be designed?
  • How many valid Internet addresses are there?

📌 Propositions — The Building Blocks of Logic

Definition (Proposition): A proposition is a declarative sentence (a sentence that declares a fact) that is either true or false, but not both.

Propositions are the basic building blocks of logic. They must be declarative statements with a definite truth value.


Not a proposition if: It is a question, a command, a request, or if its truth value depends on unspecified variables (open sentence / propositional function).

🏛️ Propositional Logic

Propositional Logic is the area of logic that deals with propositions. It was first developed systematically by the Greek philosopher Aristotle more than 2300 years ago.

Compound Propositions

One or more propositions can be combined to form a single compound proposition, using logical operators (connectives). Propositions are denoted by letters such as p, q, r, s.

Example Setup:
Let p = "It is below freezing."    Let q = "It is snowing."

Compound propositions formed:
  • a) It is below freezing and snowing. → p ∧ q
  • b) It is below freezing but not snowing. → p ∧ ¬q
  • c) It is not below freezing and it is not snowing. → ¬p ∧ ¬q
  • d) It is either snowing or below freezing (or both). → p ∨ q
  • e) If it is below freezing, then it is also snowing. → p → q
  • f) Either it is below freezing or it is snowing, but not snowing if it is below freezing. → p ⊕ q
  • g) It is below freezing is necessary and sufficient for it to be snowing. → p ↔ q

1. Negation (NOT)  ¬

Definition: Let p be a proposition. The negation of p, denoted ¬p (also denoted p̄), is the statement "It is not the case that p."

The proposition ¬p is read "not p." The truth value of ¬p is the opposite of the truth value of p.

Examples

Example 1 p: "Michael's PC runs Linux."

Simplified: "Michael's PC does not run Linux."
Example 2 p: "Vandana's smartphone has at least 32GB of memory."
¬p: "It is not the case that Vandana's smartphone has at least 32GB of memory."

2. Conjunction (AND)  ∧

Definition: Let p and q be propositions. The conjunction of p and q, denoted p ∧ q, is the proposition "p and q."

p ∧ q is true when both p and q are true, and false otherwise.

3. Disjunction / Inclusive OR  ∨

Definition: Let p and q be propositions. The disjunction of p and q, denoted p ∨ q, is the proposition "p or q."

p ∨ q is false when both p and q are false, and true otherwise. Also known as Inclusive OR.

4. Exclusive Or (XOR)  ⊕

Definition: Let p and q be propositions. The exclusive or of p and q, denoted p ⊕ q, is the proposition that is true when exactly one of p and q is true, and false otherwise.

Inclusive OR vs Exclusive OR

Inclusive OR (∨) "Students who have taken calculus or computer science can take this class."

→ Students who took both subjects are also allowed. At least one condition must be true.
Exclusive OR (⊕) "Students who have taken calculus or computer science, but not both, can enroll in this class."

→ Students who took both are excluded. Exactly one condition must be true.
Real-life example (XOR): A restaurant menu that states "Soup or salad comes with an entrée" almost always means one or the other, not both — this is Exclusive OR.

5. Conditional Statements (Implication)  →

Definition: Let p and q be propositions. The conditional statement p → q is the proposition "if p, then q."

p → q is false when p is true and q is false, and true otherwise.
Terminology
  • p = hypothesis (antecedent / premise)
  • q = conclusion (consequence)
Also called A conditional statement is also called an implication, because p → q asserts that q is true on the condition that p holds.

Type: Binary Operator  |  Symbol: →

pqp → q
TTT
TFF
FTT
FFT
Key insight: A conditional statement is only false when the hypothesis (p) is true but the conclusion (q) is false. In all other cases it is true — including when the hypothesis is false (vacuously true).
Example p: "Maria learns discrete mathematics."    q: "Maria will find a good job."

p → q can be expressed as:
  • "If Maria learns discrete mathematics, then she will find a good job."
  • "Maria will find a good job when she learns discrete mathematics."
  • "For Maria to get a good job, it is sufficient for her to learn discrete mathematics."
  • "Maria will find a good job unless she does not learn discrete mathematics."
