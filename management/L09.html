💎 Values & Policies — The Foundation

Values

Values are the foundation of ethical and responsible behaviour. They represent basic convictions about what is right and wrong.

Definition: A value is a type of belief, centrally located within one's total belief system, about how one ought or ought not to behave, or about some end-state of existence worth or not worth attaining.
  • Strong values encourage ethical conduct and social responsibility.
  • "Values tell people what is right."
Example: Dilmah Tea's core value — "Business is a matter of human service."

Policies

Policies are formal guidelines or rules created by the company to ensure everyone follows its values.

  • Rules that govern a company's code of conduct.
  • General guidelines that govern the company's behaviour.
  • Ethical values become meaningful only when supported by clear policies.
  • "Policies are the rules that make values real."
Example: Dilmah has implemented an Anti-Bribery and Corruption (ABC) Policy, Human Rights Policy, and Code of Ethics — fostering a strong sense of responsibility.

🌍 Social Obligation, Responsiveness & Responsibility

Social Obligation

The obligation of a business is to meet its economic and legal responsibilities and nothing more.

Minimum level — just follow the law and make profit.

Social Responsiveness

The capacity of a firm to adapt to changing societal conditions through the practical decisions of its managers in responding to important social needs.

React to social pressures as they arise.

Social Responsibility

A firm's obligations as a moral agent extend beyond its legal and economic obligations to the pursuit of long-term goals that are good for society.

Proactively do good for society.

"Doing good for society while doing business."

Two Views of Social Responsibility

Classical View

  • Management's only social responsibility is to maximize profits for stockholders.
  • Expending resources on "social good" unjustifiably increases costs, lowers profits, and raises prices to consumers.

Socioeconomics View

  • Social responsibility extends beyond profits to protect and enhance society's welfare.
  • Corporations are not independent — they have a moral responsibility to the broader society.
  • Firms must engage in social, legal, and political issues.
  • "To do the right thing."

🏗️ Levels of Organizational Responsibility

From the most fundamental to the most voluntary, organizations operate across four levels of responsibility:

04

Economic Responsibility Base Level

The first and most basic duty — a business must be profitable and financially stable. Without profits, a business cannot survive, pay employees, or fulfil its other responsibilities.

Examples: Dilmah producing high-quality Ceylon tea; MAS being a top global apparel manufacturer.
03

Legal Responsibility Required

The business must follow the laws and regulations of the country where it operates.

Examples: Pay taxes; Dilmah follows Sri Lankan labor laws — proper wages, working hours, and safety measures.
02

Moral Responsibility Expected

The company's duty to do what is right, fair, and just — even if no law forces them to. Acting with honesty, fairness, and care for others even when no one is watching.

Example: Dilmah ensures all workers — from tea pluckers to factory staff — are treated fairly, paid decently, and work in safe conditions.
01

Discretionary Responsibility Voluntary

Voluntary actions a business takes to help society, beyond what is legally required or expected for survival. These reflect the company's core values and differentiate it from competitors.

Example: MAS runs 'Women Go Beyond' — a program to empower female employees through education, leadership, and health initiatives.
Social Responsibility & Firm Performance: Studies suggest a positive relationship between social involvement and the economic performance of firms. A general conclusion is that a firm's social actions do not harm its long-term performance.

⚖️ Ethics

Definition: The rules and principles that define right and wrong conduct. An individual's personal beliefs about whether a behavior, action, or decision is right or wrong.
  • Business ethics refers to the moral principles and values that guide the behavior of individuals and organizations in the business world.
  • It's about doing what is right, fair, and honest — even when no one is watching — not just what is profitable or legal.
"Doing the right thing in business."
✅ Ethical Behaviour

Doing the right thing — following moral values like honesty, fairness, and respect. Actions that are good, fair, and beneficial to others.

  • Paying employees fair wages
  • Being honest with customers
  • Protecting the environment
  • Respecting laws and company rules

Example: Dilmah sells only 100% pure Ceylon tea — no mixing or cheating customers.

❌ Unethical Behaviour

Doing the wrong thing — actions that are unfair, dishonest, or harmful to people or society. Often breaks trust, rules, or moral principles.

  • Lying in advertisements
  • Using child labour or underpaying workers
  • Dumping waste into rivers
  • Accepting bribes or cheating in accounts

Example: A company hides harmful side effects of its product to keep profits high.

Reasons for Unethical Behaviour

Unawareness Unaware that the behaviour is questionable.
Lack of Standards No clear standards of conduct are in place.
Cultural Differences Different cultural norms and values across regions.
Governance Systems Behaviour-based or relationship-based governance.
Value Conflicts Different values between business people and stakeholders.

Managerial Ethics

Definition: Standards of behavior that guide individual managers in their work. Moral principles that employees in a leadership position follow to guide their actions and behaviours.
  • These ethics may be personal or related to the company's beliefs and values.
  • They help managers decide what's wrong and right so they can create a welcoming, inclusive environment.

Importance of Business Ethics

Trust & Reputation Builds trust with customers and employees.
Legal Safety Prevents legal problems and scandals.
Long-Term Success Encourages growth through fairness and transparency.
Positive Culture Creates a respectful work environment and social respect.

🏢 Organizational Culture

Definition: Organizational culture is a system of shared assumptions, values, and beliefs that governs how people behave in organizations.
  • Every company has its own unique personality — this is referred to as its culture.
  • In groups of people who work together, culture is an invisible but powerful force that influences the behaviour of members.
  • Shared values have a strong influence on how people dress and perform their jobs.
  • Every organization maintains a unique culture that provides guidelines and boundaries for the behaviour of its members.
"It's the way people think and work together in an organization."

Characteristics of Organizational Culture

Innovation Risk orientation — encourages trying new things.
Attention to Detail Precision orientation — focus on accuracy and thoroughness.
Emphasis on Outcome Achievement orientation — results matter.
Emphasis on People Fairness orientation — treating people well.
Teamwork Collaboration orientation — working together effectively.

Importance of Organizational Culture

  • Builds a sense of belonging among employees.
  • Improves teamwork and motivation.
  • Guides ethical behaviour and decision-making.
  • Helps maintain a positive company image.
  • Influences how the organization adapts to change.

🌱 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

Definition: The obligation of a business to maximize its positive impacts and minimize its negative impacts on society. CSR is a management concept that involves companies integrating social and environmental concerns into their business operations and interactions with stakeholders.
  • CSR is concerned with how an organization exceeds minimum obligations to stakeholders specified through regulation and corporate governance.
  • CSR can have a positive impact on a company's reputation, leading to better stakeholder relations, ability to attract better employees, and possibly charging higher prices.
"Doing good for people and the planet while doing business."

Triple Bottom Line (TBL)

The TBL is a model that says a company's success should not be measured only by profit, but also by its impact on people and the planet.

"A good business cares for People, Planet, and Profit."
Pillar Focus Simple Meaning Example
1. People
Social Responsibility		 Care for employees, customers, and community. Treat people fairly and improve their lives. MAS Holdings — empowers women through the "Women Go Beyond" program.
2. Planet
Environmental Responsibility		 Protect the environment and use resources wisely. Do business without harming nature. Dilmah Tea — reforestation, sustainable farming, and wildlife conservation through Dilmah Conservation.
3. Profit
Economic Responsibility		 Earn fair and sustainable profits. Make money in an ethical and responsible way. Unilever — grows business through sustainable and ethical practices.

Objectives of CSR

  • To improve the quality of life of employees and the community.
  • To protect the environment.
  • To build a positive company image.
  • To support sustainable development.
  • To create long-term business success through responsible behaviour.

Benefits of CSR

Brand Reputation Builds trust and positive public image.
Employee Loyalty Improves motivation and retention.
Customer Relations Strengthens long-term customer loyalty.
Sustainability Helps ensure long-term business success.

Is CSR Always Genuine?

Not all CSR is authentic. CSR can sometimes be driven by:
  • Propaganda — Building image without real commitment.
  • Politics — Gaining favor with governments or regulators.
  • New form of exploitation — Using CSR to mask unethical practices.
  • Social cost — The cost of CSR is passed on to consumers or employees.

Genuine CSR requires a sincere commitment to people, planet, and profit equally.

📝 Summary

"A successful organization is not judged only by profit, but also by how responsibly it acts toward people and the planet."

  • Social Responsibility — Doing good for society while doing business.
  • Business Ethics — Guiding managers and employees to do what is right and fair.
  • Organizational Culture — Shaping how people think, act, and make ethical decisions.
  • CSR — Bringing all these ideas together, balancing People, Planet, and Profit through genuine care for society and the environment.
"Good business means doing well for the company and doing good for the world."
