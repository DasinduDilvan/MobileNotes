Lesson 01 — Introduction to Management

Nature of management, key components, management functions, and types of managers.

What is an Organization?
Definition: A group of people working together in a structured and coordinated fashion to achieve a set of goals.
👥 Human ResourcesPeople, skills, talents, knowledge, abilities
💰 Financial ResourcesMoney, capital, investments, budgets
🏗️ Physical ResourcesBuildings, equipment, machinery, technology
📊 Information ResourcesData, knowledge, market intelligence
What is Management?
Ricky W. Griffin: "A set of activities (including planning and decision making, organizing, leading, and controlling) directed at an organization's resources (human, financial, physical, and information), with the aim of achieving organizational goals in an efficient and effective manner."
Mary Parker Follett: "Management is the art of getting things done through others."
F.W. Taylor: "Management is knowing exactly what you want to do and then seeing that they do it the best and cheapest way."
⚡ Efficiency"Doing things right" — Getting the most output for the least input. Focus on resources and minimizing waste.
🎯 Effectiveness"Doing the right thing" — Attaining organizational goals. Focus on outcomes and achieving objectives.
Good management requires both efficiency and effectiveness.
The 5 Management Functions
📋 Planning
→
🏗️ Organizing
→
👥 Staffing
→
🎯 Directing
→
📊 Controlling
1. PlanningDeciding in advance: what to do, how, when, and who. Base of all other functions. "Thinking before action."
2. OrganizingSetting up structure: departments, units, authority, responsibility after plans are set.
3. StaffingRight person, right job, right time. Recruit, select, train, evaluate, remunerate.
4. DirectingGuiding subordinates. Motivating, supervising, communicating, leading.
5. ControllingMatch actual vs planned performance. Find deviations, suggest corrective measures.
3 Levels of Managers
🔝 Top ManagersCEO, CFO, President, VP. Set overall goals, strategy, policies. Represent org externally. High Conceptual skills needed.
🔷 Middle ManagersPlant/dept managers. Implement top-level plans. Supervise lower managers. Balanced mix of all 3 skills.
🔹 First-Line ManagersSupervisors, foremen. Supervise operating employees. High Technical skills needed.

3 Types of Managerial Skills

💡 ConceptualThink abstractly, analyze forces, creative and innovative ability. Critical for Top Managers.
🤝 Human RelationsInteract effectively with people at all levels. Interpersonal communication and teamwork.
🔧 TechnicalUnderstanding job nature, knowledge of tasks subordinates perform. Critical for First-line Managers.
Types of Managers by Functional Area
📣 MarketingProduct development, promotion, sales, distribution
💵 FinancialAccounting, cash management, investments
⚙️ OperationsProduction, quality control, inventory control
👩‍💼 Human ResourcesPlanning, selection, recruiting, training
🏢 AdministrativeAll managerial activities; familiar with all functional areas
Nature & Characteristics of Management
  • Universal Process — applied in all institutions
  • Goal-Oriented — toward efficiency and effectiveness
  • Social Process — deals with human beings
  • Coordinating Force — avoids duplication
  • Intangible — unseen force
  • Multidisciplinary — draws from psychology, sociology
  • Dynamic — ongoing, continuous process
  • Creative Activity — encourages innovation
  • Decision-Making — continuous critical decisions
  • Profession — requires specialized knowledge

Lesson 02 — Evolution in Management

Early ideas, classical theories, behavioural approach, quantitative methods, and contemporary ideas.

Early Management Ideas (Pre-1900)
Robert Owen (1771–1858)Identified importance of working and social conditions for employees.
Charles Babbage (1792–1871)Ideas on work specialization, production efficiency, incentive and profit-sharing plans.
Henry R. Towne (1844–1924)Called for a "management science" that would establish principles of management.
Classical Approaches
1. Scientific (Taylor)
  • Select and train best workers
  • Determine most efficient work methods
  • Cooperate with workers
  • Divide work between workers and managers
  • + Gilbreth: time/motion studies
  • + Gantt: pay incentives, Gantt chart
2. Administrative (Fayol)Focused on functions of management and principles to coordinate internal activities.
  • 14 Principles of Management
  • 5 Functions: Plan, Organize, Command, Coordinate, Control
3. Bureaucratic (Weber)Org should operate rationally, not on arbitrary whims.
  • Specialization of labour
  • Formal rules & procedures
  • Impersonality
  • Well-defined hierarchy
  • Advancement on merit
Fayol's Key 14 Principles (exam favourites): Division of Work · Authority · Discipline · Unity of Command · Unity of Direction · Primacy of General Interest · Remuneration · Esprit de Corps · Centralization · Scalar Chain · Order · Equity · Low Turnover · Initiative
Behavioural Approach
Emphasizes the need to understand the effect of different factors on human behaviour in organizations.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

5. Self-Actualization — finding one's potential
4. Esteem — status, confidence, reputation
3. Belonging & Love — relationships, acceptance
2. Safety — security, stability, freedom from fear
1. Physiological — food, drink, basic well-being

McGregor's Theory X vs. Theory Y

Theory X (Scientific approach)
  • Worker is lazy, dislikes work
  • Will do as little as possible
  • Managers must supervise closely
  • Use reward and punishment
Theory Y (Behavioural approach)
  • Workers are not inherently lazy
  • Want to do a good job
  • Job itself determines attitude
  • Managers allow great latitude
Quantitative Approach (1940–Present)
Management ScienceSophisticated mathematical models and statistical methods for decision-making.
Operations ManagementManaging production and delivery of products and services.
MISDesigning and implementing computer-based information systems for management use.
Contemporary Ideas
Systems TheoryOrganizations visualized as systems with interdependent parts.
Contingency TheoryAppropriate managerial action depends on the particular situation.
Theory ZCombines positive aspects of American and Japanese management.
TQMCollective responsibility for product/service quality; everyone participates in improvement.
Evolution Timeline Summary

Lesson 03 — Business Environment

Internal and external organizational environments, PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, and adaptation strategies.

What is Business Environment?
"The business environment is the aggregate of all the conditions, events, and influences that surround and affect it." — Keith Davis
🏠 Internal EnvironmentConditions within the organization: Owners, Board of Directors, Employees, Resources, Policies, Vision/Mission, R&D, Work Environment.
🌍 External EnvironmentEverything outside the organization that might affect it. Two sub-layers: General Environment and Task Environment.
PESTLE Analysis — General Environment
🏛️ P — PoliticalGovernment policies, election results, legislation, trade agreements.
📈 E — EconomicInflation, growth rate, exchange rates, unemployment, interest rates.
👥 S — SocialDemographics, cultural trends, lifestyle changes, consumer behaviour.
💻 T — TechnologicalAI, automation, digital advancement, computer-assisted systems, Big Data.
⚖️ L — LegalRegulatory compliance, laws at local, national, and international levels.
🌱 E — EnvironmentalClimate change, sustainable practices, ecological impact.
Task Environment — 5 Specific Stakeholders
⚔️ CompetitorsOrganizations competing for same resources or customers.
🛍️ CustomersIndividuals who pay money to acquire the organization's products/services.
🔗 SuppliersOrganizations that provide resources for other organizations.
🤝 Strategic AlliesPartners in joint ventures or similar arrangements.
📋 RegulatorsUnits that can control, legislate, or influence the organization's practices.
Porter's Five Competitive Forces
🆕 New EntrantsEase of entering a market segment — threat of new competition.
⚔️ Competitive RivalryIntensity of competition between existing firms in the industry.
🔄 Substitute ProductsExtent to which competing products may replace existing ones.
🛒 Buyer PowerThe extent to which buyers can influence market rivals.
📦 Supplier PowerThe extent to which suppliers can influence market rivals.
Simple/stable environment = least uncertainty. Complex/dynamic environment = most uncertainty.
How Organizations Adapt to Their Environment
📡 Information ManagementAppoint boundary spanner, environmental scanning, establish MIS.
♟️ Strategic ResponseAlter or adopt a new strategy to align with changes.
🤝 Mergers & AlliancesCombine, purchase, or form partnerships with other firms.
🏗️ Org Design FlexibilityIncorporate flexibility in structural design to respond to conditions.
Nature of Business Environment
Complex Interdependent Dynamic Uncertain Relative

Lesson 04 — Planning & Decision Making

The planning process, organizational goals, SMART framework, types of decisions, and MBO.

What is Planning?
Planning is the primary function of management. It decides in advance: What to do · When to do it · How to do it · Who is to do what.

7-Step Planning Process

1. Need Assessment
→
2. Establish Objectives
→
3. Develop Premises
→
4. Generate Alternatives
→
5. Select Alternatives
→
6. Derivative Plans
→
7. Implement & Control
Types of Planning
Aspect Strategic Tactical Operational
Time Frame 3+ years 1–3 years Under 1 year
Responsibility Top Management Middle Management Lower-level Management
Focus Objectives, policies Procedures, projects Schedules, methods
Scope Overall company Dept integration Day-to-day ops
Organizational Goals by Level
Mission Statement — Fundamental purpose. Set by Board of Directors & Top Managers.
Strategic Goals — Long-term, comprehensive direction. Set by top management.
Tactical Goals — Medium-term, linking strategic to operational. Middle management.
Operational Goals — Short-term, day-to-day. Set by lower-level managers.
SMART Goals Framework
S — SpecificConcrete and tangible; everyone knows what success looks like.
M — MeasurableHas an objective measure of success everyone understands.
A — AttainableChallenging but achievable with available resources.
R — RelevantMeaningfully contributes to larger objectives and mission.
T — TimelyHas a deadline or timeline of progress milestones.
Decision Making
Decision making is the core of planning — the catalyst that drives the planning process.
Programmed Decisions
  • Structured, routine, recurring problems
  • Uses established criteria / precedent
  • Lower risk involved
  • More common at lower management levels
Non-Programmed Decisions
  • Unstructured, novel, non-recurring
  • Relies on subjective judgment
  • Greater risk involved
  • More common at top management level
Management by Objectives (MBO)
Process of collaborative goal setting by manager and subordinate. Extent of goal accomplishment = major factor in evaluation and reward.

Key Elements of MBO

Goal Specificity Participative Decision Making Explicit Evaluation Period Feedback

Lesson 05 — Organizing

Organizational structure, span of control, delegation, departmentalization, and new trends.

What is Organizing?
"Organizing is the process of identifying and grouping the work to be performed, defining and delegating responsibility and authority, and establishing relationships for the purpose of enabling people to work most efficiently."
DifferentiationUnits work on specialized tasks using different methods and employees with unique competencies.
IntegrationVarious units must be put back together so work is coordinated across the organization.
Process of Organizing
Identify Activities
→
Group Activities
→
Delegate Authority
→
Assign Duties
Span of Control
Narrow Span ✅
  • Close supervision and control
  • Fast communication

Narrow Span ❌
  • Many levels = high cost
  • Managers over-involved in subordinates' work
Wide Span ✅
  • Forces delegation
  • Requires clear policies

Wide Span ❌
  • Managers become decision bottlenecks
  • Risk of losing control
Tall organization = many levels (narrow span). Flat organization = few levels (wide span).
Delegation — 3 Core Elements
AuthorityPower/right to make decisions and give orders. Flows Top Down ↓
ResponsibilityDuty to perform a task assigned by a manager. Flows Bottom Up ↑
AccountabilityObligation to report back on task performance. Ensures authority and responsibility are exercised properly.
Departmentalization Bases
FunctionalGrouped by function performed (HR, Finance, Marketing).
ProductGrouped by product produced.
CustomerGrouped by common customers served.
GeographicGrouped by territory or region.
Job Design Alternatives to Specialization
Alternative Description
Job Rotation Systematically moving employees from one job to another.
Job Enlargement Giving the employee more tasks to perform.
Job Enrichment More tasks + more control the worker has over the job.
Work Team Entire group designs the work system for interrelated tasks.
Centralization vs Decentralization
CentralizationRetaining power and authority in the hands of higher-level managers. Decisions made at the top.
DecentralizationSystematically delegating power and authority throughout the organization to middle and lower-level managers.
New Trends in Organizing
Team-Based OrgWork structured around collaborative teams, not hierarchy.
Network OrgLoosely connected firms linked to share expertise and resources.
Virtual OrgExists primarily through digital connections; no central physical location.
Learning OrgContinuously learns, adapts, and improves through shared knowledge.

Lesson 06 — Leading

Leadership styles, bases of power, leadership theories, and the difference between a boss and a leader.

What is Leading?
Leading = influencing, motivating, and guiding people to achieve organizational goals.
Leading refers to the Function; Leadership refers to the Process.
5 Bases of Power
1. Legitimate PowerFrom official position in the organizational hierarchy.
2. Reward PowerAbility to give material rewards (money) or psychic rewards (recognition, praise).
3. Coercive PowerCompels compliance through threats or punishments.
4. Referent PowerBased on admiration, respect, charisma, or reputation.
5. Expert PowerComes from a person's specialized knowledge, skills, or expertise.
4 Leadership Styles
Autocratic / AuthoritarianCentralizes power. Gives orders without consulting employees. Full authority and full responsibility.
Democratic / ParticipativeDecentralizes authority. Consults subordinates. Encourages participation in decision-making.
Laissez-Faire / Free ReinAvoids power and responsibility. Passes decision-making to subordinates. Minimum initiative from leader.
Paternalistic"Papa knows best." Leader acts like head of a family — guides and protects subordinates like family members.
6 Leadership Theories
1. Great Man TheoryExcellent leaders are born, not developed. Traits: intelligence, courage, confidence, intuition, charm.
2. Trait TheoryCertain natural qualities tend to create good leaders — not every quality makes a leader.
3. Behavioural TheoryEnvironment (not natural ability) shapes leadership. Style results from responses to environment.
4. Situational TheoryA leader adapts style based on the current situation.
5. Transactional TheoryLeadership is a system of rewards and penalties.
6. Transformational TheoryLeadership results from a positive relationship between leaders and team members.
3 Leadership Skills
Technical SkillsUnderstanding work processes, activities, and technology. Specialized knowledge to perform the job.
Human SkillsDealing with people inside and outside the org. Getting along with, motivating, and inspiring people.
Conceptual Skills"Thinking in abstract terms — seeing how parts fit to form the whole."
Boss vs. Leader
😤 Boss
  • Drives employees
  • Depends on authority
  • Inspires fear
  • Says "I"
  • Places blame for breakdowns
  • Knows how it's done
  • Uses people
  • Says "Go"
🌟 Leader
  • Coaches employees
  • Based on goodwill
  • Generates enthusiasm
  • Says "We"
  • Fixes the breakdowns
  • Shows how it's done
  • Develops people
  • Says "Let's go"

Lesson 07 — Controlling

Purpose of control, the control process, types of control, and characteristics of effective control systems.

What is Controlling?
"The management function of control is the measurement and correction of the performance of subordinates in order to make sure that enterprise objectives and plans devised to attain them are accomplished." — Koontz & O'Donnell
Planning–Controlling Link: Planning and controlling are inseparable twins of management. Planning without control is meaningless. Control provides feedback for better future planning.
The 4-Step Control Process
1. Establish Standards
→
2. Measure Performance
→
3. Compare vs Standard
→
4. Take Action
Do NothingPerformance is within the acceptable range.
Correct Actual PerformanceBring performance back in line with standards.
Revise the StandardsIf standards were unrealistic or outdated.
Need for action = Desired performance − Actual performance
Types of Control by Timing
Feedforward Control(Predictive / Input Stage)
Anticipates problems before they occur. Preventive in nature.
Concurrent Control(During Process)
Corrects problems as they happen. Real-time monitoring.
Feedback Control(Output Stage / Historical)
Corrects problems after they occur. Evaluates past performance.
4 Levels of Control
Strategic ControlHow effectively strategies help the org meet its goals. Focuses on structure, leadership, technology, HR, information systems.
Structural ControlHow the org's structural building blocks are functioning. Types: Bureaucratic (formal) vs Decentralized (informal).
Financial ControlBudget, financial statements (balance sheet, income statement), ratio analysis (liquidity, profitability, activity, debt), audits.
Operational ControlDay-to-day output relative to schedules, specs, and costs. Controls flow of inputs through conversion to outputs.
Characteristics of an Effective Control System
Linked to Planning Flexible Accurate Timely Objective Forward-Looking Economical Motivating
Resistance to Control — Causes & Solutions
Common Causes
  • Overcontrol
  • Inappropriate focus
  • Rewards for inefficiency
  • Too much accountability
Ways to Overcome
  • Explain the need for controls
  • Set reasonable standards
  • Maintain flexible controls
  • Encourage employee participation
  • Associate controls with reward systems

Lesson 08 — Managerial Decision Making

The decision-making process, types, conditions, styles, and group vs individual decisions.

Core Definitions
DecisionA choice made from available alternatives.
Decision MakingThe process of identifying problems and opportunities, and resolving them.
Decision-making is the one truly distinctive characteristic of managers. It is embedded across all four management functions.
6-Step Decision-Making Process
1. Identify Problem
→
2. Set Objectives
→
3. List Alternatives
→
4. Evaluate & Compare
→
5. Implement
→
6. Follow-Up & Control
One alternative is always to do nothing. Alternatives are evaluated using criteria derived from the objective.
Decision-Making Conditions
🟢 CertaintyAlternatives and outcomes are known. Low ambiguity. Example: Purchasing a machine.
🟡 RiskProbabilities can be estimated. Moderate ambiguity. Example: Labor offer before a strike.
🔴 UncertaintyAlternatives and consequences unknown. High ambiguity. Example: Customer demand for new product.
4 Decision-Making Styles
Style Ambiguity Tolerance Way of Thinking Focus Characteristics
Directive Low Rational Task Decides quickly with limited info. Efficient but may miss nuance.
Analytical High Rational Task Gathers lots of info, very thorough and data-driven.
Conceptual High Intuitive Social Creative, broad outlook, strong long-term vision.
Behavioural Low Intuitive Social Highly people-oriented, concerned with others' wellbeing, avoids conflict.
4 Criteria for Evaluating Alternatives
PracticalCan it realistically be done?
EconomicalIs it cost-effective?
EthicalIs it morally acceptable?
LegalDoes it comply with the law?
Group vs Individual Decision Making
Group DM — Advantages ✅
  • Less victim to individual bias
  • More alternatives generated
  • Combined skills of team members
  • Greater acceptance of the decision
Group DM — Disadvantages ❌
  • Takes longer than individual
  • Therefore more costly
  • One person may dominate the group

Lesson 09 — Social Responsibility, Ethics & Culture

Foundations of social responsibility, business ethics, organizational culture, and CSR.

Social Obligation vs Responsiveness vs Responsibility
Social ObligationMeet only economic and legal responsibilities — nothing more. Minimum level.
Social ResponsivenessAdapt to changing societal conditions. React to social pressures as they arise.
Social ResponsibilityProactively do good for society. A firm's moral obligations extend beyond legal and economic duties.
Classical ViewManagement's only social responsibility is to maximize profits for stockholders. Spending on "social good" raises costs and prices.
Socioeconomic ViewResponsibility extends beyond profits to protect and enhance society's welfare. Firms have a moral responsibility to broader society.
4 Levels of Organizational Responsibility
1. Discretionary (Voluntary) — Programs to help society beyond what is required. Example: MAS "Women Go Beyond."
2. Moral/Ethical (Expected) — Do what is right, fair, and just even when no law forces it. Example: Fair wages for tea pluckers.
3. Legal (Required) — Follow all laws and regulations. Pay taxes, comply with labour laws.
4. Economic (Base) — Be profitable and financially stable. Without profit, a business cannot survive.
Business Ethics
"Doing the right thing in business — following moral values like honesty, fairness, and respect, even when no one is watching."
Ethical Behaviour ✓
  • Paying fair wages
  • Being honest with customers
  • Protecting the environment
  • Respecting laws and rules
Unethical Behaviour ✗
  • Lying in advertisements
  • Using child labour
  • Dumping waste into rivers
  • Accepting bribes
  • Hiding product side effects
CSR — Triple Bottom Line (TBL)
👥 PeopleSocial Responsibility. Care for employees, customers, and community. Treat people fairly and improve their lives.
🌱 PlanetEnvironmental Responsibility. Protect the environment. Do business without harming nature.
💰 ProfitEconomic Responsibility. Earn fair, sustainable profits. Make money in an ethical and responsible way.
Is CSR Always Genuine? Not always. CSR can sometimes be driven by propaganda, politics, or used to mask unethical practices. Genuine CSR requires a sincere commitment to all three pillars.
Organizational Culture
A system of shared assumptions, values, and beliefs that governs how people behave in organizations. An invisible but powerful force.

Key Characteristics

Innovation Attention to Detail Emphasis on Outcome Emphasis on People Teamwork

4. 🧩 Customer ExpectationDigital age + social media + COVID → demand for convenient, personalized, safe experiences. Vital for reputation and market share.

7. 🔐 Data SecurityProtect sensitive information from unauthorized access. Measures: encryption, access controls, firewalls, data backups, privacy regulation compliance.
8. 🌱 SustainabilityGrowing pressure to reduce carbon footprint, minimize waste, promote responsible sourcing. Aligned with UN's 17 SDGs.
How to Overcome Current Issues
Proactive Strategies

Lesson 11 — Contemporary Management Challenges

8 key challenges facing modern managers, their causes, importance, and potential solutions.

Why Study Contemporary Challenges?
The changing social and business environment gives rise to emerging management issues. Managers must continuously acquire knowledge to modify and adjust themselves to work efficiently in dynamic conditions.
1. 🌍 Globalization of Business
Key Competencies Required
  • Understand foreign cultures
  • Penetrate new markets
  • Design products for new customers
  • Recognize global competitors
  • Understand government intervention
Key Trends
  • Countries opening borders to foreign producers
  • Licensing and franchising expanding globally
  • Executives from host countries leading subsidiaries
  • Managers trained to think globally
2. 🌱 Environmentalism
Green movements have spread globally. Present managers face the challenge of developing their enterprise creatively, but without harming the environment. May not always be primary concern in developing countries.
3. ✅ Quality and Productivity — TQM
Quality has become one of the most competitive points in business today. Enhancing quality lowers overall cost and improves productivity.

Key TQM Principles

Beyond ProductionQuality improvement requires purchasing, marketing, after-sales service — not just production.
Coordinated EffortTotal quality through coordination of purchase, production, sales, and more.
Organization-WideEveryone and every function participates. No exceptions.
Result 1Low return of defects from customers
Result 2Minimized wastage of resources
Result 3Reduces the need for inspectors
4. ⚖️ Ethics and Social Responsibility
Code of EthicsFormal, written statement of the values and ethical standards that guide a firm's actions. Must start from top management. Covers how employees treat suppliers, customers, competitors.
Social ResponsibilityThe set of obligations an organization has to protect and enhance the societal context. Government shapes it through: direct influence (regulations) and indirect influence (taxation policies).
5. 👥 Workforce Diversity
Diversity exists when members differ along dimensions like age, gender, ethnicity. Brings a broad range of viewpoints, improves problem-solving, provides competitive advantage.
Causes of Increased Diversity
  • Globalization movements
  • Increased awareness
  • Legislation and legal action
  • Changing demographics
Contingent WorkersPart-time, temporary, or freelance employees. Estimated 13–30% of workforce. Companies downsizing permanent workforce; percentage increasing daily.
Management Shift: From treating everyone alike → recognizing differences and responding to them to ensure retention and greater productivity.
6. 🔄 Change
Managing ChangeOrganizational change must be systematic and logical. Managers must understand steps of effective change and counter resistance.
Resistance MethodsParticipation, Education, Communication, Facilitation, Support resources.
DownsizingMajor trend. Restructuring organizations. Important: work is NOT reduced — smaller workforce must complete the same workload.
7. 💪 Empowerment and Teams
Employee Empowerment: Redesigning jobs and letting individual workers and work teams make job-related decisions to improve quality, productivity, and employee commitment.
Workers are considered more knowledgeable now. Over-specialization of employees stifles their capabilities. Many organizations achieve better results by empowering employees to plan and execute their own work.
8. 🏪 Shift Towards a Service Economy
The service sector has become increasingly important. There is a significant difference between managing manufacturing organizations and service organizations — managers must adapt their approach accordingly.
Potential Solutions to Management Challenges
📅 PlanningProactive and strategic planning to anticipate and respond to challenges.
⏱️ Time ManagementEffective allocation and prioritization of managerial time.
🤝 A Good TeamBuilding a skilled, collaborative, and diverse team.
💰 Cash FlowMaintaining healthy financial operations under pressure.
🔍 Competition AnalysisContinuous study of the competitive environment.
