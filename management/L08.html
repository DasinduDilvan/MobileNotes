Managerial Decision Making

Lecture 08  |  Management Studies

📋 Learning Outcomes

  • Explain the meaning and significance of managerial decision-making.
  • Identify the steps of the decision-making process.
  • Define the different types, conditions, and styles of decision-making.
  • Describe the importance of effective decision-making.

🔄 Four Functions of Management (Context)

Planning Choose Goals
Organizing Working together
Leading Coordinate
Controlling Monitor & Measure
Decision-making is embedded across all four management functions — it is what makes managerial action possible.

📖 Core Definitions

Decision A choice made from available alternatives.
Decision Making The process of identifying problems and opportunities, and resolving them.
Decision-Making Process: Recognizing and defining the nature of a decision situation, identifying alternatives, choosing the best alternative, and putting it into practice.

Many decisions that managers deal with every day involve at least some degree of uncertainty and require non-programmed decision-making.

⭐ Significance of Decision-Making

  • Decision-making is the one truly distinctive characteristic of managers.
  • Decisions made by top managers commit the entire organization toward particular courses of action.
  • Decisions made by lower levels of management implement the strategic decisions of top managers in the operating areas.
  • Decisions invariably involve organizational change and the commitment of scarce resources.

🔍 Characteristics of Managerial Decision-Making

Goal-Oriented Every decision is directed toward achieving specific objectives.
Choice Among Alternatives Involves selecting from two or more possible options.
Judgment & Rationality Requires reasoned thinking as well as intuition.
Uncertainty & Risk Outcomes are rarely fully predictable.
Continuous Process Decision-making is ongoing, not a one-time event.

🔁 Model of the Decision-Making Process

1. Identify Existing Problem
→
2. Set Managerial Objectives
→
3. List Alternative Solutions
→
4. Select Most Beneficial Alternative
→
5. Implement Chosen Alternative
→
6. Feedback & Control

Step 1 — Identifying the Existing Problem

A problem is a discrepancy between an existing and a desired state of affairs.

Step 2 — Setting Managerial Objectives

  • Objectives constitute the foundation for rational decision-making.
  • Objectives are the ends for the means of managerial decision-making.
  • Attainment of the objective is the ultimate measure of decision success.

Step 3 — Searching for Solutions

Information is rarely complete. Key constraints on the search include:

  • Limitations of time and money
  • The declining value of additional information
  • The rising cost of additional information
  • Maintaining cost-effectiveness
Why Information is Incomplete:
  • Uncertainty and risk
  • Time and cost constraints
  • Ambiguous information

Step 4 — Comparing & Evaluating Alternatives

  • Alternatives result from the search; usually three to five alternatives exist.
  • One alternative is always to do nothing.
  • Alternatives are evaluated using criteria derived from the objective.
  • Evaluation should include an anticipation of the likely outcome for each alternative.
  • Evaluation should also anticipate obstacles or difficulties at the time of implementation.
General Criteria for Evaluating Possible Courses of Action:
Practical Can it realistically be done?
Economical Is it cost-effective?
Ethical Is it morally acceptable?
Legal Does it comply with the law?

The Act of Choice

  • The choice is the culmination of the process, not all of it.
  • The choice confronts the decision-maker with discernible constraints.
  • The best alternative may not be readily apparent to the decision-maker.
  • The best choice is likely to ensue from the right approach.
  • The choice should be the alternative most likely to result in attaining the objective.

Step 5 — Implementing Decisions

Decision success is a function of decision quality AND decision implementation.

Step 6 — Follow-Up and Control

  • Follow-up and control are essential to ensure an implemented decision meets its objective.
  • Performance is measured by observing the implemented decision against its standard derived from the objective.

📂 Types of Decisions

Programmed Decisions A decision that is fairly structured or recurs with some frequency (or both).
Example: Purchasing materials for production; standard organizational transactions.
Non-Programmed Decisions A decision that is relatively unstructured and occurs much less often.
Example: Decisions on new facilities, new products, labor contracts, and labor issues.

Decision Type vs. Management Level

Management Level Dominant Decision Type
Top Management Mostly Non-Programmed (strategic)
Middle Management Mix of both
Lower Management Mostly Programmed (operational)

As management level increases → non-programmed decisions increase; programmed decisions decrease.

🌡️ Decision-Making Conditions

The decision maker can face three types of conditions, ranging from lower to higher ambiguity and risk of a bad decision:

Certainty 🟢

The decision maker knows with reasonable certainty what the alternatives are and what conditions are associated with each alternative.

Example: Purchasing a machine from a supplier.

Ambiguity: Lower

Risk 🟡

The availability of each alternative and its potential payoffs and costs are all associated with risks (probabilities can be estimated).

Example: Labor offer from a labor union before a strike.

Ambiguity: Moderate

Uncertainty 🔴

The decision maker does not know all the alternatives, the risks associated with each, or the consequences of each alternative.

Example: Customer demand for a new product.

Ambiguity: Higher

🧠 Decision-Making Styles

Styles are defined along two dimensions: tolerance for ambiguity (low/high) and way of thinking (rational/intuitive).

Directive

Low ambiguity tolerance Rational thinking

Task-focused. Makes decisions quickly using limited information. Efficient but may overlook nuance.

Analytical

High ambiguity tolerance Rational thinking

Task-focused. Gathers more information, carefully analyzes before deciding. Thorough and data-driven.

Conceptual

High ambiguity tolerance Intuitive thinking

Social-focus. Broad, creative outlook. Considers many alternatives; strong long-term vision.

Behavioural

Low ambiguity tolerance Intuitive thinking

Social-focus. Highly people-oriented. Concerned with others' well-being; avoids conflict.

Rational Intuitive
Low Ambiguity Directive Behavioural
High Ambiguity Analytical Conceptual

🔭 Scope of Decision-Making

Individual Decision Making

A single manager makes the decision independently.

Group Decision Making

Decisions are made collectively by a team or committee.

Organizational Decision Making

Decisions affect and involve the organization as a whole.

👥 Group Decision Making

Decisions made by groups rather than individuals. Group decision making is generally considered superior to individual decision making because:

  • Choices are less likely to fall victim to bias.
  • Able to draw on the combined skills of group members.
  • Improves ability to generate feasible alternatives.
  • Allows managers to process more information.
  • Managers affected by decisions are more likely to agree to cooperate.
✅ Advantages
  • More alternatives are likely to be generated.
  • More acceptance of the final decision is likely.
  • Can identify and evaluate more alternatives.
❌ Disadvantages
  • The process takes longer than individual decision making.
  • Therefore, it is costlier.
  • One person may dominate the group.

✅ Essentials for Effective Decision-Making

Accuracy Decisions must be based on correct and reliable information.
Environment for Decision A supportive organizational climate is required.
Timely Decision Decisions must be made at the right time — not too early or too late.
Communication The decision must be clearly communicated to all concerned.
Participative Decision Making Involving relevant stakeholders improves quality and acceptance.
Implementation Even a good decision fails without proper execution.

⚠️ Difficulties in Decision-Making

  • Incomplete information — Full data is rarely available.
  • Unsupportive environment — Organizational culture may resist good decisions.
  • Non-acceptance by subordinates — Decisions can fail without buy-in.
  • Ineffective communication — Poorly conveyed decisions lead to misunderstanding.
  • Incorrect timing — Decisions made too early or too late lose their value.

🎓 In-Class Activity (SGD)

Topic: "Should Sri Lanka encourage more electric vehicle imports?"

Each group should apply the decision-making process:

  • Identify the problem — What issue is being addressed?
  • Generate alternatives — What are the possible courses of action?
  • Evaluate pros & cons — Apply the four criteria (Practical, Economical, Ethical, Legal).
  • Make a group decision — Select the best alternative.
  • Present findings — Share conclusions with the class.
