Lesson 04 - Functions of Management

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the functions of decision-making and the planning process.
  • Understand organizational goals and objectives.
  • Identify the components of organizational planning.

What is Planning?

Definition: Planning is an intellectual process of thinking resorted to deciding a course of action that helps to achieve the pre-determined objectives of the organization in the future.
  • Planning is the primary function of management.
  • It is the outline of activities to be done in the future toward predetermined goals.
  • It is the process of setting goals and choosing the means to achieve those goals.
  • It defines goals and takes the necessary steps to ensure those goals are achieved.
  • It determines the organization's goals and selects a course of action within both the internal and external environment.
Planning decides in advance:   What to do  ¬∑  When to do it  ¬∑  How to do it  ¬∑  Who is to do what

Purpose of Planning

Achieve ObjectivesDirects all efforts toward clear, pre-set organizational goals.
Cope with UncertaintyPrepares the organization for future changes and challenges.
Facilitate ControlProvides a standard to measure actual performance against.
Help in CoordinationAligns activities across different departments and units.
Guide Decision-MakingGives a framework for choosing the best course of action.
Increase EffectivenessImproves overall organizational performance and output.

On Plans and Planning

  • A plan is a predetermined course of action to achieve stated goals.
  • Plans are based on forecasts and assumptions about the future.
  • Plans need to be flexible so they can adapt to inevitable changes in the environment.
  • The simpler the plan, the better.

The 7-Step Planning Process

Planning Process Flow

Need Assessment ‚Üí Establish Objectives ‚Üí Develop Premises ‚Üí Generate Alternatives
‚Üí Select Alternatives ‚Üí Formulate Derivative Plans ‚Üí Implementation & Control
  1. Need Assessment Identify the "organizational need." This could result from an opportunity or a problem faced by the organization.
  2. Establish Objectives Define what the organization hopes to achieve. Answer the question: "What do we want to accomplish?"
  3. Develop Premises Planning premises are the assumptions about the environment in which plans will be made and implemented.
  4. Generate Alternatives There are multiple methods to achieve any objective ‚Äî enlist all possible alternatives. Methods: Brainstorming, Nominal Group Techniques, Delphi Method.
  5. Select Alternatives Filter alternatives against organizational objectives. Conduct feasibility studies and cost-benefit analysis. Select the optimum alternative(s) for implementation.
  6. Formulate Derivative Plans Secondary plans addressing finer implementation at department and unit levels. Indicate timelines and schedules clearly.
  7. Implementation and Control Execute the plan to achieve set goals. Simultaneous monitoring and control are necessary to stay on track.

Methods of Planning

According to the usage and nature of planning, methods are divided into 3 categories:

Objective Plans Objectives are treated as basic plans. Basic plans are necessary for all types of planning operations.
Standing Plans Include policies and procedures. Apply to repetitive actions. Provide a ready guideline for recurring problems. Limit managerial freedom to ensure integrated and cooperative actions.
Master Plans Covers the complete course of action along with consideration of time and strategy. May be concerned with production, sales, purchase, and similar activities.

What is Decision Making?

"It's in your moments of decision that your destiny is shaped."
Definition: Decision making is the part of the planning process that involves selecting a course of action from a set of alternatives.
  • A decision is a choice from available alternatives.
  • It is a process, not an event.
  • It is the core of planning.
  • It is the cornerstone of planning ‚Äî the catalyst that drives the planning process.
  • Decision making underlies every aspect of setting goals and formulating plans.

Organizational Goals

Definition: Organizational goals are strategic objectives that a company's management establishes to outline expected outcomes and guide employees' efforts.
Purpose of Goals:
  • Provide guidance and a unified direction for people in the organization.
  • Affects the quality of other aspects of planning.
  • Source of motivation to employees.
  • Provide an effective mechanism for evaluation and control.

Kinds of Goals

By Level:
Mission StatementFundamental purpose of the organization. Set by Board of Directors & Top Managers.
Strategic GoalsSet by and for top management. Long-term, comprehensive direction for the whole organization.
Tactical GoalsSet by and for middle management. Medium-term; links between strategic and operational.
Operational GoalsSet by and for lower-level managers. Short-term, specific, day-to-day focus.
By Area: Goals can be set for different functional areas of the organization (marketing, finance, HR, operations, etc.)
By Time Frame:   Long-term (beyond 3‚Äì5 years)  ¬∑  Intermediate-term (1‚Äì5 years)  ¬∑  Short-term (up to 1 year; explicit or open-ended)

Organizational Planning

Definition: Setting goals, establishing strategies, and developing plans to coordinate activities.
  • All organizations plan, but not in the same fashion.
  • All planning occurs within an environmental context.
  • All goals require plans to guide in their achievement.
  • All goals are tied to higher goals and plans.

Contingency Planning

Definition: The determination of alternative courses of action to be taken if an intended plan is unexpectedly disrupted or rendered inappropriate.

Why Planning is Important

  • Focus attention on appropriate goals.
  • Improve employee motivation.
  • Helps identify managerial talents.
  • Evaluate progress and change approaches as needed.
  • Facilitates control of the organization.
  • Enhances communication across all levels.

Advantages & Limitations of Planning

Advantages
  • Helps in achieving objectives
  • Better utilization of resources
  • Reduces uncertainty and risk
  • Improves competitive strength
  • Effective control and co-ordination
  • Encourages motivation
  • Provides decentralization; improves efficiency
  • Anticipation of crisis
Limitations
  • Lack of accurate information
  • Time and cost; inflexibility
  • Delay during the emergency period
  • False sense of security

Barriers to Goal Setting & Planning

Barriers
  • Inappropriate goals
  • Improper reward system
  • Changing environment
  • Reluctance to establish goals
  • Resistance to change
  • Constraints / Limitations
Overcoming Barriers
  • Understand the purpose of goals and plans
  • Communication and participation
  • Effective reward systems
  • Regular revision and update

Objectives

Definition: Objectives are the aims, purposes, or goals that an organization wants to achieve over varying periods of time.
Important Characteristics of Objectives:
  • Objectives have a hierarchy
  • Objectives form a network
  • Multiplicity of objectives
  • Objectives have a time span
  • Objectives may be tangible or intangible
  • Objectives must have social sanction
Significance of Objectives: Unified planning  ¬∑  Defining an organization  ¬∑  Direction  ¬∑  Individual motivation  ¬∑  Basis for decentralization  ¬∑  Basis for control  ¬∑  Co-ordination

Management by Objectives (MBO)

Definition: The process of collaborative goal setting by a manager and subordinate. The extent to which goals are accomplished is a major factor in evaluating and rewarding the subordinate's performance.
How MBO Works:
  • Specific performance goals are jointly determined by employees and managers.
  • Progress toward accomplishing goals is periodically reviewed.
  • Rewards are allocated based on progress towards the goals.
Key Elements of MBO:   Goal specificity  ¬∑  Participative decision making  ¬∑  An explicit performance/evaluation period  ¬∑  Feedback

Benefits & Weaknesses of MBO

Benefits
  • Improvement of managing
  • Clarification of organization
  • Personal satisfaction
  • Teamwork
  • Development of effective control
  • Fast decision making
Weaknesses
  • Failure to teach the philosophy of MBO
  • Failure to give guidelines to goal-setters
  • Difficulty in setting goals
  • Emphasis on short-term goals
  • Danger of inflexibility
  • Time-consuming
  • Increased paperwork
