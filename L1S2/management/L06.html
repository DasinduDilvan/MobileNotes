Functions of Management

Lecture 06 - Leading

Learning Outcomes

  • Define the Leading and Leadership
  • Define the Leader and Leadership styles
  • Compare and contrast early theories of Leadership
  • Discuss the importance of Leadership

What is Leading?

  • Leading is a management function that involves influencing others to engage in the work behaviors necessary to reach organizational goals.
  • Leading is the process of influencing, motivating, and guiding people to achieve organizational goals.
  • Leading refers to the Function, and Leadership refers to the Process.

Leading as a Process

  • Developing vision and goals
  • Communicating the visions
  • Motivating and inspiring people
  • Building teams and relationships
  • Guiding and directing
  • Monitoring and feedback
  • Recognizing and rewarding

How Leaders Influence Others

  • Managers are expected to maintain effective workforces.
  • To be able to do so, they are required to perform leadership roles.
  • Leaders are said to be able to influence others because of the power they possess.
  • Power refers to the ability of a leader to exert force on another.

Bases of Power

1. Legitimate Power

  • A person who occupies a higher position has legitimate power over persons in lower positions within the organization.
  • Legitimate power comes from a person's official position or authority in the organizational hierarchy.

2. Reward Power

  • When a person has the ability to give rewards to anybody who follows orders or requests.
  • The ability to give benefits, incentives, or recognition to others in return for compliance with requests or orders.
  • Two forms of rewards:
    • Material reward – refers to money or other tangible benefits
    • Psychic reward – consists of recognition, praise

3. Coercive Power

  • When a person compels another to comply with orders through threats or punishments.
  • The leader forces compliance by using threats, punishments, or penalties if people do not follow instructions.
  • Example: A lecturer tells students that if they miss too many classes, they will lose exam eligibility.

4. Referent Power

  • When a person can get compliance from another because people see you as a reference for something else, bigger than yourself.
  • Based on admiration, respect, or identification with a leader.
  • It comes from the leader's charisma, personality, or reputation.

5. Expert Power

  • Provides specialized information regarding their specific lines of expertise. People with great technology skills possess it.
  • Expert power comes from a person's knowledge, skills, or expertise.

The Nature of Leadership

  • One cannot expect a unit or division to achieve objectives in the absence of effective leadership.
  • Even if a leader is present, if he is not functioning properly, no unit or division objectives can be expected to be achieved.

Leadership

  • Leadership is the art of motivating the actions of others
  • Leadership is doing the right things
  • Leadership is setting a clear direction in the face of uncertainty
  • Leadership is the ability to exude confidence and attract followers
  • Leadership is the demonstrated ability to innovate, make decisions, influence, and motivate to achieve results

Leader

  • A person or thing that leads; a guiding or directing head.
  • A person who rules, guides, or inspires others.
  • One whom others follow.

Traits of Effective Leaders

  • A high level of personal drive
  • The desire to lead
  • Personal integrity
  • Self-confidence
  • Analytical ability or judgment
  • Knowledge of the company, industry, or technology
  • Charisma
  • Creativity
  • Flexibility

Leadership Behaviors

Job-Centered Leader Behavior

  • The behavior of leaders who pay close attention to the job and work procedures involved with that job.

Employee-Centered Leader Behavior

  • The behavior of leaders who develop cohesive work groups and ensure employee satisfaction.

Ways Leaders Approach People

Positive Leadership

  • When the leader's approach emphasizes rewards.

Negative Leadership

  • When punishment is emphasized by the leader.

Leadership Skills

Leaders need to have various skills to be effective.

1. Technical Skills

  • These are skills a leader must possess to enable them to understand and make decisions about work processes, activities, and technology.
  • Technical skill is specialized knowledge needed to perform a job.

2. Human Skills

  • These skills refer to the ability of a leader to deal with people, both inside and outside the organization.
  • Good Leaders must know how to get along with people, motivate them, and inspire them.

3. Conceptual Skills

  • These skills refer to "the ability to think in abstract terms, to see how parts fit together to form the whole".
  • A leader without sufficient conceptual skills will fail to achieve this.

Additional Leadership Skills

  • Decisiveness – Effective leaders are those who can make decisions quickly with the information they have.
  • Integrity – Truthfulness or honesty
  • Relationship building (or team building) – The ability to build and maintain a strong, collaborative team of individuals working toward the same goal.
  • Problem-solving – Good leaders are skilled at solving issues that arise on the job.
  • Dependability – People can trust and rely on you.
  • Ability to teach and mentor – Leaders think less about themselves and more about how to make their team as a whole successful.

How to Be a Leader

  • Be an empathetic leader
  • Be a good listener
  • Be a model for embracing change
  • Incorporate a shared vision
  • Develop your followers
  • Communicate realistic and clear expectations
  • Learn how to handle failure
  • Continue the education

Leadership Styles

1. Autocratic or Authoritarian Leadership

  • An autocratic leader centralizes power and decision-making in himself.
  • He gives orders, assigns tasks, and duties without consulting the employees.
  • The leader takes full authority and assumes full responsibility.

2. Democratic or Participative Leadership

  • Participative or democratic leaders decentralize authority.
  • It is characterised by consultation with the subordinates and their participation in the formulation of plans and policies.
  • He encourages participation in decision-making.

3. The Laissez-Faire or Free Rein Leadership

  • Free-rein leaders avoid power and responsibility.
  • The laissez-faire or non-interfering type of leader passes on the responsibility for decision-making to his subordinates and takes a minimum of initiative in administration.
  • He gives no direction and allows the group to establish its own goals and work out its own problems.

4. Paternalistic Leadership

  • The leader assumes that his function is fatherly or paternal. Paternalism means "Papa knows best".
  • The relationship between the leader and his group is the same as the relationship between the head of the family and the members of the family.
  • The leader guides and protects his subordinates as members of his family.

Leadership Theories

1. The Great Man Theory

  • The excellent leaders are born, not developed.
  • Characteristics: Intelligence, courage, confidence, intuition, and charm, among others.

2. The Trait Theory

  • Certain natural qualities tend to create good leaders.
  • Characteristics: Some leaders may be excellent listeners or communicators, but not every listener or communicator makes an excellent leader.

3. The Behavioural Theory

  • How a person's environment, not natural abilities, forms him or her into a leader.
  • Characteristics: A person will be more likely to act or lead in a certain style as a result of environmental responses to behaviour.

4. The Situational Theory

  • A leader is one who is able to adapt their style based on the situation.

5. The Transactional Theory (or Management Theory)

  • Leadership is a system of rewards and penalties.

6. The Transformational Theory (or Relationship Theory)

  • Leadership is the result of a positive relationship between leaders and team members.

Importance of Leadership

1. Initiates Action

  • Leader is a person who starts the work by communicating the policies and plans to the subordinates from where the work actually starts.

2. Motivation

  • A leader proves to be playing an incentive role in the concern's working.
  • He motivates the employees with economic and non-economic rewards.

3. Providing Guidance

  • A leader has to not only supervise but also play a guiding role for the subordinates.

4. Creating Confidence

  • Confidence is an important factor that can be achieved through expressing the work efforts to the subordinates, explaining to them clearly their role, and giving them guidelines to achieve the goals effectively.

5. Building Morale

  • Morale denotes the willing co-operation of the employees towards their work, getting them into confidence, and winning their trust.

6. Builds Work Environment

  • Management is getting things done from people.
  • An efficient work environment helps in sound and stable growth.
  • He should have personal contacts with employees and should listen to their problems and solve them.
  • He should treat employees on humanitarian terms.

7. Co-ordination

  • Co-ordination can be achieved through reconciling personal interests with organizational goals.

Key Distinction: Boss vs. Leader

Boss:

  • Drives employee
  • Depends on authority
  • Inspires fear
  • Says, "I"
  • Places blame for the breakdown
  • Knows how it is done
  • Uses people
  • Takes credit
  • Commands
  • Says, "Go"

Leader:

  • Coaches them
  • On goodwill
  • Generates enthusiasm
  • Says, "We"
  • Fixes the breakdowns
  • Shows how it is done
  • Develops people
  • Gives credit
  • Asks
  • Says, "Let's go"

Key Takeaway

"You don't have to be great to start, but you have to start to be great."
