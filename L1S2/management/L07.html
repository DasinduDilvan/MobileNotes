Functions of Management

Lecture 07 - Controlling

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the meaning of control
  • Explain the purpose of control
  • Describe the steps in the controlling process
  • Identify the different types of control
  • Identify and explain the characteristics and resistance of control

Concept of Controlling

  • Controlling refers to the process of measuring performance according to standards, measuring deviations, and taking corrective action to achieve predetermined goals.
  • To seek results, the manager must exercise effective control over the workers.

Definition

"The management function of control is the measurement and correction of the performance of subordinates in order to make sure that enterprise objectives and plans devised to attain them are accomplished."

— Koontz & O'Donnell

Henry Fayol's Definition

Control consists of verifying whether everything occurs in conformity with the plan adopted, the instructions issued, and the principles established.

What is Controlling?

  • A process of monitoring, comparing, correcting performance, and taking action to ensure desired results
  • It sees to it that the right things happen, in the right ways, and at the right time
  • In order to seek results, manager must exercise effective control over the workers

Control

  • The regulation of organizational activities in such a way as to facilitate goal attainment
  • The regulation of organizational activities so that some targeted performance elements remain within acceptable limits

Nature and Characteristics of Control

  • A function of management
  • A dynamic process
  • A continuous activity
  • A pervasive function
  • Forward-looking
  • A clear relationship between control and planning

Purpose of Control

Adapt to environmental change
Limit the accumulation of error
Minimize costs
Cope with organizational complexity
Control helps the organization

Main Value of Controlling

The value of the control function can be identified in three important areas:

  • Planning – control provides feedback for better planning
  • Empowering employees – control enables employee autonomy with accountability
  • Protecting the workplace – control ensures safety and security

Importance of Controlling

  • Accomplishing organizational goals
  • Judging the accuracy of standards
  • Making efficient use of resources
  • Improving employee motivation
  • Ensuring order and discipline
  • Facilitating coordination in action

Planning and Controlling are Interrelated and Interdependent

  • Planning and controlling are inseparable twins of management
  • They are interrelated and interdependent functions of management
  • Without planning there lies no basis for controlling and vice versa
  • Planning is the basis of controlling functions
  • Planning without control is meaningless
  • Planning is a thinking process while controlling is an executive function
  • Planning based on facts makes controlling easier and more effective
  • Controlling improves future planning by providing information derived from past experiences

The Control Process

  • The control process is a three-step process of:
    • Measuring actual performance
    • Comparing actual performance against the standard
    • Taking managerial action to correct deviations or address inadequate standards
  • The control process assumes that performance standards already exist, and they do

Steps in the Control Process

Establish Standards → Measure Performance → Compare Against Standards → Determine Need for Action

↓

Do Nothing | Correct Deviation | Change Standards

Step 01: Setting Performance Standards

Standards are the criteria, against which actual performance would be measured. These serve as benchmarks, toward which an organization strives to work.

Functional Area Standards Used
Production Quality, quantity, cost, individual job
Marketing Sales volume, sales expenses, advertising expenditure, sales person performance
HR Labour relations, labour turnover, labour absenteeism
Finance & Accounting Capital expenditure, inventories, flow of capital, liquidity

Step 01: Measurement of Actual Performance

  • Once the standards are fixed, the next step is to measure the actual performance
  • Generally, it is conducted by the managers to analyze the overall efficiency level of the employees
  • While measuring performance, objective and reliable methods should be used
  • Four approaches managers use to measure and report actual performance:
    • Personal observations
    • Statistical reports
    • Oral reports
    • Written reports

Step 02: Comparing

  • The comparing step determines the variation between actual performance and a standard
  • Identification of the acceptable range of variation
  • Comparison becomes easier when standards are set in quantitative terms

Analyzing Deviations

Under this step, deviations in the key areas of the business need to be attended to on an urgent basis, as compared to deviations in certain insignificant areas. There is a need to determine the acceptable range of deviations in all operational areas.

Need for action = Desired performance – Actual performance

Step 03: Taking Managerial Action

Three possible courses of action for managers:

  • Do nothing – performance is within acceptable range
  • Correct the actual performance – bring performance back in line
  • Revise the standards – if standards are unrealistic

Types of Control: Areas of Control

Physical Resources

Inventory management, quality control, and equipment control

Human Resources

Selection and placement, training and development, performance appraisal, and compensation

Information Resources

Sales and marketing forecasts, environmental analysis, public relations, production scheduling, and economic forecasting

Financial Resources

Managing capital funds and cash flow, collection, and payment of debts

Level of Control

01. Strategic Control

  • Focus on how effectively the organization's strategies are succeeding in helping the organization meet its goals
  • Focuses on five aspects of organizations:
    • Structure
    • Leadership
    • Technology
    • HR
    • Informational and Operational system

02. Structural Control

  • Monitors how the building blocks of the organization's structure are holding up to their planned function
  • Two major types of structural control:
    • Bureaucratic Control – formal and mechanistic structural arrangements
    • Decentralized Control – informal and organic structural arrangements

03. Financial Control

  • The control of financial resources as they flow into the organization
  • Involves costs and expenses to control them in relation to budgeted amounts
  • Tools of financial control:
    • Budget – planned allocation of resources
    • Financial statements (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow)
    • Ratio analysis (Liquidity, profitability, activity, debt)
    • Financial audit (internal and external)

04. Operational Control

  • Concerned with executing the strategy
  • Regulates the day-to-day output relative to schedules, specifications, and costs
  • Controls flow of inputs (natural resources, raw materials, human resources, capital) through conversion process to outputs (products and services)

Types of Control: By Timing

Feedforward Control (Predictive)

  • Occurs at the input stage
  • Anticipates problems before they occur
  • Preventive in nature

Concurrent Control

  • Occurs during the process/implementation stage
  • Corrects problems as they happen
  • Real-time monitoring

Feedback Control (Historical)

  • Occurs at the output stage
  • Corrects problems after they occur
  • Evaluates past performance

Control Techniques

Traditional Techniques

  • Personal observation
  • Statistical reports and analysis
  • Break-even analysis
  • Budgetary control

Advanced Techniques

  • Self-control
  • Management audit
  • Programme evaluation and review techniques (PERT)
  • Critical Path Method (CPM)
  • Management Information System (MIS)

Requirements of an Effective Control System

  • Focus on objectives and needs
  • Corrective actions
  • Suitability
  • Simple
  • Forward-looking
  • Control by exception
  • Flexibility
  • Motivating
  • Economical

Characteristics of Effective Control

  • Integration with planning – closely linked to planning function
  • Flexibility – adaptable to changing conditions
  • Accuracy – precise measurement and reporting
  • Timeliness – prompt reporting and action
  • Objectivity – impartial and fact-based

Resistance to Control

Common sources of resistance:

  • Overcontrol
  • Inappropriate focus
  • Rewards for inefficiency
  • Too much accountability

Ways to Overcome Resistance to Control

  • Explain to workers the need for controls
  • Change in workers' attitude
  • Implement realistic control
  • Set reasonable standards of performance
  • Revaluation of controls periodically
  • Proper focus of attention on key areas
  • Maintain flexible controls
  • Encourage participation of employees in control processes
  • Controls should be associated with a system of reward
  • Define appropriate range of control

Key Takeaway

"You can not always control what goes on outside, but you can always control what goes on inside"
